# Overview

## HAIN in one sentence

HAIN is a Hybrid AI Native framework for thinking about cloud, edge, and agentic systems through clearer public structure, cleaner architectural language, and structured narrative assets.

## What HAIN World is

HAIN World is the public-facing identity of the HAIN framework.

It is designed to function as a structured entry point rather than as a conventional product brand. Its role is to make the broader HAIN concept understandable, navigable, and publicly presentable through websites, terminology, repository structure, and narrative materials.

In practical terms, HAIN World serves as the umbrella layer that connects the main site, the architecture layer, and the future toolkit direction.

## Why HAIN exists

AI systems are no longer defined by a single deployment model or a single execution environment.

Cloud infrastructure continues to matter for scale, orchestration, and shared intelligence. Edge execution matters because locality, latency, privacy, and deployment realism have become important design conditions. Agentic systems matter because intelligent software is increasingly expected to coordinate tools, workflows, memory, and actions across environments.

These shifts create a need for better language and clearer structure. HAIN exists to provide a more organized way to describe this landscape.

## Core structure

The current HAIN framework is structured around three layers:

### HAIN World
The public entry point and umbrella identity for the framework.

### HAINOA
The open architecture layer. This is where the framework becomes more explicit, structured, and interpretable.

### HAINIZE
The toolkit and developer-oriented entry. This layer points toward reusable assets, templates, and implementation-oriented materials.

## Current intent

At its current stage, HAIN is not presented as a fully commercialized platform. It is being developed as a structured conceptual framework, a public-facing architecture narrative, and a curated set of naming and documentation assets.
